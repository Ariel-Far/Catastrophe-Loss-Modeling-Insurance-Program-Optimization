How a 100,000-year simulation turned a three-way standoff between a CFO, a risk manager, and a broker into a single, defensible insurance decision.

i.The Engagement
A real-estate portfolio, three ways to lose money

Montgomery Realty holds commercial assets concentrated in three metros — each exposed to a different catastrophe. Their broker had procured three insurance programs with different retentions, limits, and premiums. The question looked simple: which one? It wasn't. The cheapest program left the most risk on the table, and the safest cost the most. Choosing well required modeling losses that haven't happened yet.

ii.The Method
From 240 historical claims to 100,000 simulated years

Rather than trust a single average, I built the full distribution of what a year could cost — then tested every program against all of it.

01

Model the severity of a single loss

Fit three candidate distributions to the historical fire losses and selected the best by formal goodness-of-fit — AIC and the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test — not by eye.

Lognormal · KS p = 0.43 · only candidate to pass
02

Simulate 100,000 years of losses

Combined claim frequency with fitted severity in a Monte Carlo engine — fire from the data, windstorm and earthquake from catastrophe-model inputs — to build each peril's full annual loss curve.

Poisson frequency · Pareto cat-severity
03

Stress-test every program

Ran all three insurance structures against the simulated losses — applying retention, per-occurrence and aggregate limits, and coinsurance — to see what the client truly retains.

Retained loss · TCOR · TVaR

My Recommendation
I introduced a fourth lens the brief didn't ask for — Tail Value at Risk, the average cost of the worst years. It revealed that Insurer 3 caps catastrophic exposure at roughly a quarter of the others'. Where every standard metric optimizes the typical year, TVaR prices the year that ends a balance sheet. I recommended Insurer 3 for a risk-averse client, with Insurer 1 as the cost-efficient alternative — and showed the client exactly what the premium difference buys.

Why this is the part that matters

A lower average is not a lower risk. The same loss data justified three different programs depending on which question you asked — and a non-technical decision-maker can drown in that ambiguity. The job wasn't to run the simulation; it was to give the client a clear, defensible answer and show them precisely what they were buying with every premium dollar. That translation — from heavy-tailed distributions to a decision a CFO can sign off on — is the work.

Methodology, in brief. Severity fitting via maximum likelihood with AIC / Kolmogorov–Smirnov selection; collective-risk Monte Carlo over 100,000 simulated years; program stress-testing on retained loss, Total Cost of Risk, and Tail Value at Risk.
